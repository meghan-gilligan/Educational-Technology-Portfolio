01 — About

A practitioner who
designs for the person
in front of them

My work has always been about meeting people in the middle of change — a young person navigating their first job search, an adult returning to learning after years away, a student stepping into professional practice for the first time. For more than fifteen years, I have worked at the intersection of education, employment, and human development, and what has remained constant across every role is a belief that good learning design begins with deep respect for the learner.

I began my career as an employment counsellor, holding a diploma in social service work and eventually building a practice that spanned youth programming, career coaching, transition support, and community development. Over time I became the person organizations called on not just to deliver programs, but to design them.

My undergraduate degree in educational studies, with a specialization in adult learning and technology, gave me language and theoretical grounding for what I had been doing intuitively. I now approach curriculum and learning design with both practice and principle — using frameworks like backward design, grounding assessment in clearly defined learning outcomes, and drawing on experiential learning theory.

I am drawn to roles in post-secondary education and learning design where I can contribute to programs and experiences that take equity seriously — built around who learners actually are, not who institutions assume them to be.

02 — Philosophy

How I think
about learning

"When you design for the person who has the most barriers, you build something better for everyone."

Meeting people where they are

Effective adult learning begins with the learner, not the curriculum. I approach every design challenge by first asking who is in the room, what they already know, and what barriers might stand between them and the learning. This isn't just good pedagogy — it's respect.

Equity as a design principle

Inclusive design isn't an add-on or an accommodation — it's foundational. My experience working with learners across a wide spectrum of abilities, backgrounds, and life circumstances has shaped a practice that centres access, dignity, and belonging from the outset.

Bridging theory and practice

I use frameworks like backward design and experiential learning theory not as rigid templates but as lenses. The goal is always transfer — creating learning experiences that connect meaningfully to what people will actually do when they leave the room.

Lifelong learning as a lived value

Returning to university while working full-time wasn't just a credential — it was a commitment to practising what I teach. I believe educators who continue to learn alongside their students bring a different quality of presence to the work.
